Hi, I am Fang Yuan, a postdoctoral research fellow at the The Australian National University and the ARC Centre of Excellence for All-sky Astrophysics (CAASTRO). I am interested in a range of cosmic explosion phenomena, e.g. supernova and gamma-ray bursts.

I grew up in Ningbo, a city on the southeastern coast of China. I got my undergraduate degree in physics from Peking University in 2003 and completed my PhD in physics at the University of Michigan in 2010.

I study cosmic explosions with SkyMapper, a 1.3m robotic telescope at the Siding Spring Observatory in Australia. SkyMapper is making the first comprehensive digital map of the entire southern sky (known as the Southern Sky Survey) and a time-lapse movie of approximately 1000 square degree every three to four days (known as the SkyMapper Supernova Survey). I use computer software to find stars that change in brightness, in particular, supernovae, the violent explosive deaths of stars. When an interesting supernova is found, I observe it with bigger telescopes to try tounderstand its origin and explosion physics. A special type of supernova, namely Type Ia, has been used to discover the accelerating expansion of the Universe. Our survey will discover about 50 Type Ia supernovae every year, enabling us to improve the constraints on dark energy.
Some transients are short lived and first discovered in wavelengths other than optical. Optical observations, however, provide critical clues about their nature. A good example is gamma-ray bursts, the most energetic explosions known after the big bang. During my PhD, I used ROTSE, a set of robotic telescopes, to catch the fast decaying (in minutes) optical emission from gamma-ray bursts. I continued to observe gamma-ray bursts with SkyMapper, using a mode that will override any other onging observations. I also point SkyMapper to the mysterious fast radio bursts and the gravitional wave events, recently detected for the first time by the LIGO detectors.

Light emitted from a distant galaxy or an exploding star appears to have an increased wavelength (or a redder color). This so-called redshift, is a key ingredient for mapping the 3D structure of the Universe. I am part of the OzDES team to use the multi-fibre optical spectrograph (2dF + AAOmega) at the Anglo-Australian Telescope to measure redshifts for tens of thousands of galaxies observed by the Dark Energy Survey. These redshifts will be used to make the most detailed measurement of the Universe's expansion history ever and will lead to a better understanding of the physics behind the acceleration of the Universe.
My favorite programming language is Python. I have co-developed a pipeline to process SkyMapper data and find supernovae, using mostly Python, some C and open-source modules. I use Django (a Python Web framework) to build and manage database for survey data from SkyMapper and OzDES. I have taught introductory Python to Astrophysics graduate students.

During my PhD, I used IDL extensively to analyze astronomical data and I've written an image subtraction software in IDL. I also use Perl and Shell script for automated data handling.

I have developed MATLAB scripts to process high frequency trading data for financial research.

I am organizing CAASTRO in the Chinese Classroom, a program bringing together CAASTRO scientists and Chinese high school students in virtual classrooms. So far, we have participating schools from Beijing, Hangzhou, Guangzhou and Akesu.
